Should code be 100% free?

We firmly believe that code should be 100% free for hobbyists, small companies and nonprofits. Price should never stop anyone from building stuff. Period. We love building too much to ever compromise on that.

On the other hand, we have large companies who want two things: open source and a way to pay for software. They prefer paying for well-maintained tools rather than using free but unreliable software.

At the end of the day, a well designed financial model free for everyone except large companies can be a win for everyone. And, more importantly, it can be a win for collaboration; a financial model that preserves forkability does not hinder collaboration but actually fosters it.

We do share the dream of a society where code is always free but the reality is that we live in capitalism where money is required to live and develop software. If someday our society evolves and money is not required anymore, whether it be with a basic income or something else, then we'll sunset the Lsos.

Most software today is proprietary: Facebook, Gmail, Google, YouTube, Windows, Zoom, Reddit, and even dating apps. These have profound impacts on our lives and we should try to create open-source alternatives. But to do so, we need novel ways to finance ambitious open source projects.

Bringing open source and financial values together while preserving forkability can make a profound contribution to our quest to achieve the dream of an open, collaborative, kind, and explorative world.

If you have alternative ideas, we are all ears. But we will not follow the ones who scream "No! Code should be always free even for large companies!" while not proposing a viable alternative. Blocking all ideas doesn't help the many open source developers who struggle financially. If we are on a ship heading towards an iceberg, we shall not listen to those who say "don't change anything". If no one suggests an actionable alternative course of actions, we will stick to our plan of bringing large companies to pay.

We keep control over the Lsos within people we trust, and we don't bring investors in we don't fully trust. We ask you to trust us. Can you trust an unknown organization? Definitely not. Is it worth the risk to trust us? Maybe. Personally, I'd rather take risks in my life than never moving forward.

Maybe we will never achieve our dream and that's okay. We will help the tens of thousands of open source developers to earn a living from what they love doing, and, if we can enable even only a couple to work more on open source, we will have made the world a little bit of a better place.
